Role of Museums in the Parisian Culture

Paris is a cultural destination par excellence, with an astonishing number of 136 museums. These museums display a wide range of historical, scientific, and archaeological artifacts from around the world, covering diverse and unique topics including fashion, theater, sports, and more. The city's museums are not only home to regular gallery spaces but also host important art exhibitions, making them a hub for art and culture enthusiasts. In 2008, Paris accounted for three of the 10 most visited museums in the world, with the Louvre ranking first, on par with London and more than all other cities.


Paris' museum system mainly reflects the city's history in many different aspects and angles, with collections that have been donated to the city and are now displayed at museums such as the Cernuschi and Cognacq-Jay, partly reflecting the life and society in Paris centuries ago. The artifacts owned by the city of Paris can be divided into four collections: fine arts, fine arts and clothing, archeology and history, and finally artifacts.  The city of Paris currently manages 14 museums and display venues, and unlike the national museums, all the city's museums do not charge the public for admission. 


Along with its rich museum offerings, Paris is also known for its world cultures. Museums like the Guimet, Cernuschi, and Ennery unveil the richness of their collections of Asian art, while the Musée de l’Homme focuses on the evolution of man and societies. The city's history museums offer an incredible journey through time, with the Musée d'Archéologie national, the Musée de Cluny, the Musée Carnavalet, and the Archaeological Crypt on the square in front of Notre Dame Cathedral, all providing insights into Prehistory, Antiquity, and the Middle Ages in France, as well as the origins of the French capital.
